Choosing a custom development agency is a high-stakes decision. You are committing MYR 50,000 to MYR 250,000 to a team you probably just met, trusting them to deliver something you cannot yet see, over a timeline that will span 3-6 months or more.

Get the decision right and you have a competitive advantage for years. Get it wrong and you lose time, money, and often the goodwill of your team and stakeholders.

Here is a practical guide to evaluating agencies without getting burned.

## The three things that actually matter

Marketing agencies will tell you a hundred things matter. In practice, three things predict whether your project will succeed:

1. **Have they done this before** — with similar companies, similar complexity, similar technologies?
2. **Do they communicate clearly and honestly** — even when the news is uncomfortable?
3. **Can you live with them for 3-6 months** — because you are about to?

Everything else is secondary. A beautiful portfolio does not matter if they cannot communicate. A great sales pitch does not matter if they have never built anything like your project. A low price does not matter if you spend the next 6 months managing them instead of running your business.

## Questions that reveal real capability

During discovery calls, most agencies will tell you what they want you to hear. These questions tend to reveal what they are actually like to work with:

### "Can you show me a project that went wrong, and how you handled it?"

Every agency has had projects go wrong. The ones worth hiring will tell you honestly and describe the specific steps they took to recover. Agencies that claim they never have problems are either lying or inexperienced.

### "What would cause you to walk away from this project?"

Good agencies have standards. They will tell you about clients they have declined, projects they have ended early, or scope creep they have refused. Agencies that will "do anything" often do it poorly.

### "How do you handle scope changes mid-project?"

Scope changes are inevitable. The right answer is not "we do whatever the client wants" — that path leads to endless projects and blown budgets. The right answer involves a clear change request process, written approval, and transparent cost adjustments.

### "Who specifically will work on my project, and can I meet them?"

Many agencies pitch with their senior team, then assign the work to juniors. Insist on meeting the actual people who will design and code your project. Their experience and communication style matter more than the founder's.

### "What happens if I want to end the relationship early?"

Legitimate agencies have clear termination terms, hand over all code and assets, and do not hold your project hostage. Agencies that evade this question are waving a red flag.

## Red flags that predict disaster

Some warning signs are so reliable that hitting even one should give you pause.

### They quote a fixed price without discovery

Any agency that gives you a quote after a 30-minute call is either wildly overpricing to cover unknown risk, or they have not understood your project and will inevitably hit scope issues. Real discovery takes multiple conversations, documentation review, and sometimes workshops.

### They pressure you with urgency

"This price is only good this week." "We have a slot opening next month." "If we do not start soon, we cannot hit your timeline." These are sales tactics. Legitimate agencies book projects based on capacity, not closing pressure.

### They badmouth competitors

Professionals talk about their own work, not others' failures. An agency that spends time criticising other agencies in the discovery call will probably talk about you the same way when you leave.

### Their own website is bad

If their website is slow, outdated, or poorly designed, how confident are you they can build you a good one? Look especially at their Core Web Vitals, their case studies, and whether their own portfolio pieces are still live.

### They cannot explain their process

"We are very agile" is not a process. A real process has defined phases, deliverables at each phase, client review points, and clear expectations for both parties. Ask them to walk you through their process step by step — if they cannot, they do not have one.

### They have no written proposal

A reputable agency produces a detailed written proposal after discovery. It should include scope, timeline, phases, deliverables, assumptions, exclusions, and total cost. Anything less is setting you up for misalignment.

## What to look for in proposals

When you receive written proposals, evaluate them against these criteria:

- **Specificity.** Does it describe your specific project, or could it apply to anyone?
- **Assumptions stated clearly.** Does it list what they assume you will provide?
- **Exclusions stated clearly.** Does it say what is NOT included?
- **Phased payment terms.** Is payment tied to milestones, not just time?
- **Post-launch support.** What happens after launch?
- **Intellectual property.** Who owns the code and content?
- **Revision rounds.** How many rounds of changes are included?

A proposal that skips these details will turn into a dispute later.

## Red flags in pricing

Watch for these pricing patterns:

- **Suspiciously low.** An agency quoting 50% below others is either desperate, incompetent, or planning to make it up in change orders.
- **Suspiciously high.** Without clear justification, high pricing may mean they are covering for inefficiency or padding margins.
- **Vague line items.** "Development: MYR 50,000" with no breakdown is a recipe for conflict.
- **No breakdown by phase.** How will you know what you are paying for at each stage?

## What good agencies do differently

After years of working with different agencies, clients consistently identify these qualities in the good ones:

- They ask more questions than you expected
- They push back on ideas that will not work
- They admit when something is outside their expertise
- They respond to emails within 24 hours
- They give bad news quickly and directly
- They take ownership when things go wrong
- They celebrate your wins, not just their work

## Making the final decision

After discovery calls and proposals, you typically narrow to 2-3 finalists. The final decision rarely comes down to price or technical capability — it usually comes down to fit. Which team do you trust? Which communication style works for you? Which process gives you confidence?

Trust your instincts on this. You are going to spend months with these people. If something feels off in the sales process, it will feel worse six weeks in.

## A final thought

The best agencies are often booked 2-6 months out. If you find one you trust and they have availability, say yes. If they are booked, consider waiting. Working with a great agency 3 months from now is better than starting with a mediocre one next week.
